🚀 Project Overview: Exoplanet Hunter

The Mission: Turning Curiosity into Discovery

We believe science should be open to everyone. Our project transforms real NASA Kepler data into an interactive platform where anyone can try to detect exoplanets. With the help of machine learning, users can experience the thrill of discovery while contributing to real science. Every star hides a story. Some hide entire worlds. Our neural network helps reveal them — distinguishing true planetary signals from the background noise of the cosmos.

The Simple Idea: The Transit Method

When a planet passes (or "transits") in front of its host star from our perspective, the star's brightness dips slightly. This is the transit method, the most effective way to find new planets. However, not every dip in brightness is a planet. Signals are often obscured by:

  • False positives: Distant binary stars or eclipsing star systems.
  • Instrument errors: Noise from the telescope.
  • Statistical variation: Random fluctuations in light.

Our model automates this process, sifting through millions of data points to find subtle patterns in numerical features that are nearly impossible for the human eye to spot.

🔭 Learn More: The Science and the Solution

The Data: Kepler Objects of Interest (KOI)

Our model is trained on the Kepler Objects of Interest (KOI) Catalog, an immense list of "suspicious" objects compiled by the Kepler Space Telescope. For years, Kepler continuously monitored thousands of stars, recording the slightest variations in their brightness. Each time the light dimmed, an entry was added to the database: a potential planet transit.

However, in this cosmic lottery, not every dimming event signifies an exoplanet. The data is categorized with labels essential for our training:

  • Confirmed Exoplanet: A verified world.
  • Candidate: An object with transit-like features, requiring further study.
  • False Positive: An event caused by something other than an exoplanet.

We specifically focused on training our neural network to distinguish between Candidates and False Positives, learning where a planet is truly hiding versus where it's merely an optical illusion.

The Transit Method Explained

The transit method is perhaps the most elegant way to find planets beyond our system. It's based on a remarkably simple principle: If a planet crosses directly in front of its star from an observer's perspective on Earth, the star's measured brightness drops slightly.

Imagine you're looking at a lightbulb, and a tiny speck of dust briefly passes in front of it. The light doesn't vanish, but it becomes marginally dimmer. Kepler did the same thing, but instead of dust specks, it was looking for Earth- or Jupiter-sized planets orbiting stars hundreds of light-years away.

How It Works:

  • Measurement: The telescope continuously measures the light intensity of thousands of stars.
  • Detection: If brightness periodically drops for the same duration and depth, it indicates an orbiting planet.
  • Analysis: The shape of the light "dip" reveals key properties: orbital period, size, distance, and temperature.

Our tool is designed to be an assistive technology, not a replacement for human astronomers. It helps them filter the immense haystack of data to rapidly pinpoint the few "pearls"—objects that truly merit closer inspection.
